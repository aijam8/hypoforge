# Research notes — superconductivity feature study

## Goal
Understand which structural/compositional features most raise the superconducting
critical temperature (Tc), and find a *testable* direction we could actually pursue.

## What we have (lab context)
- University condensed-matter group, modest budget.
- **Synthesis:** box + tube furnaces, arc melter, ball mill (polycrystalline bulk samples).
- **Characterization:** powder XRD; Quantum Design PPMS (resistivity, magnetization, heat
  capacity down to 1.8 K, fields to 9 T); SEM/EDS via shared facility.
- **Compute:** a small workstation; occasional access to a campus HPC allocation for DFT.
- **We do NOT have:** a diamond-anvil cell / high-pressure capability (no >~2 GPa),
  no thin-film MBE, no neutron source, no synchrotron of our own.

## Observations from our data so far
- Cuprates dominate the high-Tc ambient-pressure regime; all contain Cu and are layered perovskites.
- The hydrides (H3S, LaH10) reach the highest Tc but only under ~150–170 GPa.
- Iron-based superconductors are layered and reach moderate Tc; FeSe jumps dramatically as a monolayer.
- Pressure clearly matters (Hg-1223 rises 134 K → 164 K under 30 GPa).

## Open question
Is there an ambient-pressure, layered-materials route to higher Tc that our lab could
actually synthesize and measure, rather than chasing high-pressure hydrides we can't make?
